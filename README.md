## How the Mechanism Actually Works (Not Marketing)
Sodium Alginate is not a novel or unproven ingredient. Alginate raft therapy is an established, clinically studied approach to managing gastro-oesophageal reflux. It is the same mechanism used in pharmacy products like Gaviscon Advance (used widely in the UK and Australia). When Sodium Alginate contacts stomach acid, it undergoes a chemical reaction with naturally occurring calcium ions to form a viscous, buoyant gel — a physical raft that floats on top of stomach contents. This raft acts as a mechanical barrier, not a chemical one. It does not suppress acid production (unlike PPIs) and does not neutralise acid (unlike antacids). It simply prevents acid from reaching the oesophagus by blocking the path physically. This mechanism is particularly effective at night because lying flat removes the gravitational advantage that keeps acid in the stomach during the day. Studies on alginate raft therapy include randomised controlled trials published in peer-reviewed journals. The mechanism is not in dispute — it is the same one used in regulated pharmacy products across multiple countries.

The formulation contains exactly three ingredients, each with a defined role.

Objection: "PPIs already handle reflux. Why would someone need this?"
Response: PPIs reduce acid production but do not eliminate it. Residual acid remains in the stomach and, when a person lies flat, can still rise into the oesophagus — this is why many PPI users still experience night-time symptoms. Alginate therapy addresses the physical movement of acid, not its volume. The two mechanisms are complementary, not competing. There are no known interactions between Sodium Alginate and proton pump inhibitors.

Objection: "Why would someone with Barrett's oesophagus use a supplement?"
Response: Barrett's oesophagus is caused and worsened by repeated acid exposure to the oesophageal lining over time. While no supplement reverses Barrett's, reducing the frequency and duration of acid contact with the oesophagus is considered a meaningful part of conservative management alongside PPIs and surveillance endoscopy. Reflux Shield provides an additional mechanical barrier during the night — the highest-risk period for acid exposure — as a complement to, not a replacement for, medical treatment.
